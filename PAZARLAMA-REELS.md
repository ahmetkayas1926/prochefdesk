## Küme 1 — Menü mühendisliği (Star / Plowhorse / Puzzle / Dog)

### 1. The dish you're afraid to touch
There's usually one dish you won't touch. It sells every service, the regulars order it, so you leave it exactly as it is — same price, same portion, for years. That's the dangerous one. Because while the dish stayed frozen, everything inside it moved. The protein went up. The oil, the garnish, the packaging. Cost climbs a few cents at a time, and nobody notices a few cents. The menu price hasn't moved in two years. The cost has. So the dish that feels like your safest earner is often the one with the thinnest margin on the whole menu — and because it sells the most, you're losing those thin margins a hundred times a night. Popular doesn't mean profitable. Sometimes popular just means the leak is bigger. *[On my own [dish], when I finally costed it properly, the margin was [X] — far less than I'd assumed.]* The dishes you're afraid to touch are exactly the ones to cost first.

### 2. Before you cut the slow seller
Before you pull a dish that doesn't sell, check what it actually makes you. Some of the slowest sellers on a menu are the most profitable plates you have — high margin, low volume. The instinct is to cut them because they don't move. But a dish that sells five a night at a strong margin isn't a failure. It's a dish nobody's been given a reason to order. The problem usually isn't the dish — it's where it sits on the menu, how it's described, whether the staff ever mention it. So before it goes, try moving it up the page, rewriting the line, telling the floor to recommend it for a week. You might be sitting on your best margin and treating it like a mistake. Slow doesn't mean unprofitable. Sometimes it just means unseen.

### 3. Where a dish sits changes what it earns
People don't read a menu top to bottom. Their eyes land in a few spots — the top of a section, the first and last item, anything boxed or set apart. So the position of a dish quietly decides how often it sells, before the food has anything to do with it. Most kitchens build the menu around how the food is grouped — starters here, mains there — and never think about which slots actually get seen. But if your highest-margin main is buried in the middle of a list of eight, you're hiding your best earner. And if a thin-margin dish sits in the most-read spot, you're pushing volume onto the plate that makes you the least. You don't always need to change the food. Sometimes you just need to move it. A menu isn't a list. It's a layout — and the layout has a cost.

### 4. Not every weak dish should be cut
The menu math says the dish that sells little and earns little should go. Usually true — but not always. Some weak sellers earn their place off the numbers entirely. A dish that uses up a trim you'd otherwise bin. One that lets you order a whole animal or a full case instead of a portion at a premium. The option that doesn't sell much but keeps a table of four from walking out. Cut that on the numbers alone and you can lose more than you save. So when a dish looks like a dog, ask one more question before it goes: what is it doing for the rest of the kitchen? If the answer is nothing, cut it. If it's quietly holding up your ordering or your yield, the spreadsheet isn't seeing the whole plate.

## Küme 2 — Gerçek maliyet / yield

### 5. Two chefs cost the same dish and get different numbers
Give the same recipe to two chefs and ask them what it costs. You'll often get two different numbers — and both think they're right. The gap is almost never the ingredients. It's the assumptions. One costs the beef at the price on the box. The other costs it after trimming, knowing a third of that piece never reaches a plate. One ignores the oil, the salt, the garnish because they're cheap. The other knows cheap things, multiplied across every cover, stop being cheap. One uses last month's invoice. The other uses this week's, because the price moved. A food cost isn't one number you find once. It's a set of honest assumptions you keep current. The chef with the higher number usually isn't pessimistic. They're just the one telling the truth about yield, waste and time.

### 6. Your dish costs more than it did last month
The cost you wrote on a dish is a photograph, not a fact. It was true the week you costed it. Since then the protein moved, the oil moved, the supplier slipped a few cents onto a line you never check. None of it is dramatic — that's the problem. Nothing moves enough to notice, so nobody looks. But across a menu, across a season, those quiet moves stack up, and the food cost you're managing to is a number from months ago. You're steering with an old map. You don't need to recost everything every week. You need the cost to recalculate when a price changes — so the day the supplier moves, every dish that uses it moves with it. A food cost isn't something you do once a year at budget time. It drifts. And drift is where the margin goes.

### 7. The food cost number nobody likes
There's a food cost number most kitchens avoid, because it's higher than the one they tell themselves. It's the real one — costed with the waste in. The trim you cut off and bin. The portion remade because it wasn't right. The end of the service pan that goes home as staff food. None of that is on the recipe, so none of it is in the cost — but you paid for all of it. Put it back in, and your food cost climbs a few points, and a few of your safe dishes stop looking safe. That number is uncomfortable. It's also the only one that matches what actually leaves your bank account. You can manage to the clean number on the recipe, or the real number with the waste in. Only one of them is true.

## Küme 3 — İşçilik maliyeti

### 8. The cheapest dish on your menu might be the most expensive
There's a dish on most menus with a beautiful food cost. Three or four dollars on a plate you sell for eighteen. On paper, the star of the menu. But it takes one cook twenty minutes of prep before service, every single day. That time never shows up in food cost — so the dish looks like a winner while it quietly spends your most expensive resource: your team's hours. A plate isn't cheap because the ingredients are cheap. It's cheap when it's cheap to get onto the pass — ingredients and labour. The dish that costs four dollars in food and forty minutes in hands might be more expensive than the one that costs eight in food and two minutes to plate. Cost the time, not just the box.

### 9. When making it yourself costs more
"We make it in-house" sounds like the right answer. Sometimes it's the expensive one. Take something you make from scratch that you could buy ready — a stock, a sauce, a base. Making it feels cheaper because the ingredients are cheap. But making it costs hands, and a station, and oven space, and the hours of a person who could be doing something only your kitchen can do. Add the labour and the loss, and the thing you're proud to make from scratch can land higher than buying it in at a price that made you wince. In-house is worth it when it's better, or when it uses time you'd be paying for anyway. It's not automatically cheaper just because you didn't buy it. Cost the hands, not just the ingredients — then decide.

### 10. Why the special quietly costs the most
The special is where labour hides. It's not on a kitchen card yet, the portion isn't locked, and it usually takes more hands than your regular menu because it's new every time. So the dish you're running to use up product, or to look creative, can quietly be the most expensive thing leaving your kitchen that week — not in ingredients, in time. None of it shows up where you'd look, because specials rarely get costed at all; they come and go too fast. But a special that runs three days, prep-heavy, on a busy section, can cost you more in hours than it ever brings in at the price you guessed that morning. Specials are worth running. They're just worth costing too — at least the labour — before you decide the price on the fly.

## Küme 4 — Fire / waste

### 11. Over-portioning is a price rise you didn't decide
Every plate that goes out a little heavier than the recipe is a food cost increase you never approved. It feels generous, and on one plate it's nothing — a few extra grams of protein, a heavier pour of oil, a garnish by feel instead of by weight. But the recipe is the only place your cost is real, and the moment the plate stops matching the recipe, your real cost drifts above the number you're managing to. Multiply a few grams by a few hundred covers a week and you've raised your own food cost by points — without raising a single price to cover it. Consistency isn't about being mean with portions. It's about your cost on paper matching the plate that actually leaves. If the plate's heavier than the recipe, the recipe is fiction — and so is your food cost.

### 12. The trim in your bin is money you already paid for
You paid for the whole thing. The whole fish, the whole case, the full bunch. But a lot of it never reaches a plate — it gets trimmed, and the trim goes in the bin. That bin isn't waste in the abstract. It's money you already spent, thrown out twice: once when you binned the product, and again when you buy more to replace what the trim could have become. The bones that could be stock. The trim that could be a staff meal or a second dish. The stalks, the offcuts, the ends. None of it has to be glamorous. It just has to not be bin. Reducing waste isn't a sustainability slogan in a kitchen. It's refusing to pay for the same product twice.

### 13. Your food cost looks fine and your bin is full
You can hit your food cost target on paper and still be bleeding money — and the proof is in your bin, not your spreadsheet. Food cost percentage measures what you sold against what you bought. It doesn't see what you bought and threw away before it ever became a sale. So a kitchen can run a clean food cost number while binning trim, over-prepping, and tossing the end of every pan — because all of that is buried inside "what you bought," not flagged as loss. The percentage looks healthy. The waste is invisible to it. If your numbers say one thing and your bin says another, believe the bin. It's the only number that doesn't round.

## Küme 5 — Tedarik / satın alma

### 14. Your supplier raised a price and didn't tell you
Suppliers don't call to tell you a price went up. It just appears on the next invoice, on one line, among forty others. A few cents here, a dollar there — never enough to argue about, never enough to notice. So it doesn't get noticed. Meanwhile every dish that uses that product is now costing more than the recipe says, and the recipe doesn't know, because nobody updated it. Weeks later your food cost is two points higher and you're hunting for a reason. The reason was on an invoice you scanned and filed. You don't catch this by reading every line every week. You catch it when a price change updates the cost of every dish that uses it — so the leak shows up the day it starts, not the quarter it adds up. The invoice is where margin walks out, one quiet line at a time.

### 15. Ordering by feel versus ordering by number
Most ordering happens by feel. You look in the walk-in, you get a sense of what's low, you call it in. It works, until it doesn't — until you over-order something that spoils, or run out mid-service of something you swore you had. A par level replaces the feeling with a number: this is how much we hold, this is the line, below it we order back up to it. It sounds rigid. It's actually what lets you stop thinking about it. The feeling has to be relearned by every new person; the number is the same for everyone, on every shift, whether you're there or not. Ordering by feel is a skill that lives in one head. Ordering by number is a system that survives a day off.

### 16. The bulk deal that costs you money
A good price on a big quantity isn't a good deal if half of it ends up in the bin. The unit price drops, so it feels like a win — buy the bigger case, save a few percent. But the saving is only real if you use it before it turns. Buy two weeks of a perishable to save five percent, lose a quarter of it to spoilage, and you didn't save anything — you paid more, and threw away the difference. The deal that matters isn't the lowest price per kilo. It's the lowest price per kilo you actually serve. Cheap to buy and cheap to use are not the same number. Before you take the bulk price, ask whether you'll plate it all — or just store it until it's waste.

## Küme 6 — Fiyatlandırma

### 17. Raising a price without losing the table
You can usually raise a price more than you think, as long as you do it where it won't be felt. Fifty cents on a main is invisible to almost every guest — nobody walks because a dish went from nineteen to nineteen-fifty. But across a few hundred covers a week, that fifty cents is real money, and it often does more for your margin than cutting cost ever could. The fear is that the table notices and resents it. They rarely do, on a small move, on a dish they came for. What they notice is a sudden jump, or a cheaper plate, or a portion that shrank. So the move isn't to hike everything — it's to nudge the dishes that can carry it, by an amount nobody feels. Price is a lever you're allowed to pull. Most kitchens are just scared to touch it.

### 18. Pricing off your competitor is a trap
Looking at what the place down the road charges feels like research. It's mostly a trap. Their price is built on their costs — their suppliers, their yields, their rent, their labour, their volume. None of which are yours. So when you match their price, you inherit a number that was never calculated for your kitchen. Maybe they buy at a scale you can't. Maybe they're losing money on that dish and don't know it. Maybe they're about to close. Copying the price copies their math, blind. Your price has to start from your cost and the margin you need to survive — then you glance at the market to sanity-check it, not to set it. The competitor's menu tells you what they charge. It tells you nothing about what you can afford to.

### 19. When food cost percentage is the wrong target
Food cost percentage is a useful number that becomes a trap when it's the only one. Chase a low percentage and you'll quietly favour the wrong dishes. A plate that's thirty-five percent food cost but takes forty minutes of labour can make you less than a plate that's forty-five percent and goes out in two. The percentage looked worse and the dish made more — because percentage doesn't see time, and time is most of your cost. What pays the rent isn't a low percentage. It's cash margin per plate, against the hours of your kitchen it took. Percentage is a guardrail, not a goal. Manage to it blindly and you'll cut the dishes that actually feed your business.

## Küme 7 — Tutarlılık / standardizasyon

### 20. The dish changes when you're not there
If a dish tastes different on your day off, that's not a staff problem. It's a recipe problem. When the only place the real recipe lives is in your head, every plate is a guess by whoever's on — a pinch, a feel, "about that much." On a good day it's close. Over a week, with three different cooks, it drifts, and the guest who came back for the dish they had last time gets a different one. The fix isn't watching the section harder. It's getting the dish out of your head and onto a card — weights, not handfuls; steps, not memory — so the plate is the same whether you're on the pass or asleep. Consistency isn't talent. It's the recipe being written down well enough that talent isn't required to repeat it.

### 21. Write the recipe for the cook who's never made it
Most kitchen recipes are written for the person who already knows the dish — a list of ingredients and a few words, because the rest is "obvious." It's only obvious to you. The recipe that actually protects the dish is written for the cook who's never made it: the weights, the order, the thing that goes wrong if you rush the step everyone rushes. It feels like over-explaining. It's the opposite — it's the dish surviving a new starter, a busy section, a day you're not there. A recipe isn't a reminder for you. It's an instruction for someone who doesn't have your hands. Write it for them, and the dish stops depending on who's holding the pan.

### 22. The card that ends the questions
You can measure how good a recipe is by how many questions it stops. If the section is still asking how much salt, how long, how big the portion — the recipe isn't finished, no matter how detailed it looks. A real kitchen card answers the question before it's asked, at the station, where the hands are, mid-service when there's no time to find you. That's not extra admin. That's hours of your week back — every question you're not answering, every plate you're not re-checking, every "is this right?" that never reaches you. The point of writing it down isn't neatness. It's that the dish runs without you in the loop. The best card is the one nobody needs to ask you about.

## Küme 8 — Uyum / HACCP

### 23. The HACCP log isn't for the inspector
Everyone treats the temperature log as a thing you do for the inspector — a box ticked so nobody gets in trouble. That's the least important reason to keep it. The real reason is the day something goes wrong. A guest gets sick, a complaint comes in, a batch is questioned — and the only thing standing between you and "we don't know" is a record. With a log, you can show the fridge was at temperature, the cook hit the number, the cooling happened on time. Without it, you're defending your kitchen from memory, and memory doesn't hold up. The log isn't paperwork to keep the inspector happy. It's the proof that protects you when something's already happened and there's no inspector in the room. You're not logging for them. You're logging for the day you'll be very glad you did.

### 24. The cooling step most kitchens get wrong
Cooking kills the bacteria. Cooling badly brings them back — and it's the step most kitchens get wrong, because it happens after service when everyone wants to go home. Hot food left to cool on its own sits for hours in the exact temperature range where bacteria multiply fastest. The rule exists for a reason: down through the danger zone fast, not slowly overnight in a deep covered pot. Split it into shallow trays, get it moving, hit the marks within the window. The cooked dish that made someone sick usually wasn't undercooked. It was cooled slow, stored warm in the middle, and reheated like it was fine. Cooking is the part everyone respects. Cooling is the part that actually catches kitchens out.

### 25. "We've always done it this way" fails an audit
Every kitchen has a way it's always done things. Some of it is wisdom. Some of it is a habit nobody's questioned, that works right up until the day it's tested. An audit — or worse, an incident — doesn't care how long you've done it that way. It asks whether it's safe, and whether you can show it. "We've always done it like this" is not an answer to either. The habits worth keeping are the ones you could explain to an inspector and back with a record. The ones you keep only because they're old are the ones that catch you out. Tradition is fine in a kitchen until it's the reason something goes wrong. Then it's just a thing you can't justify.

## Küme 9 — Prep / mise

### 26. The prep list is a cost-control tool
A prep list looks like a to-do list — make this, chop that, fill these. But it's quietly one of the strongest cost controls you have. Prep too much and the surplus turns at the end of the week and goes in the bin: money made, then thrown away. Prep too little and you're 86'ing dishes mid-service, or someone's pulled off the line to make more at the worst possible time. The right prep list isn't "everything we might need." It's how much you'll actually sell, made to that number — tied to the bookings, the covers you expect, the days you're closed. Get it right and waste drops, labour smooths out, and nothing sits in the walk-in dying. A prep list isn't a chore. It's you deciding, in the morning, how much money to spend on the day.

### 27. Making too much costs as much as making too little
Both ends of batch sizing cost you. Make too little and you run out — lost sales, a cook pulled to remake it under pressure, a dish off the menu at the busy time. Make too much and the extra sits, ages, and gets binned at the end of the week — product and labour, both paid for, both thrown out. The instinct is always to make plenty, because running out feels worse in the moment than waste does later. But waste is the more expensive mistake, because you paid to make it before you threw it away. The right batch isn't "a bit extra to be safe." It's the number you'll actually use before it turns. Safe isn't making more. Safe is making the right amount and trusting the number.

### 28. Your mise is money already spent
Every container on your mise station is money you've already spent, sitting there waiting to become a sale or become waste. We don't think of it that way — prep feels like getting ready, not like spending. But the moment you portioned it, cut it, cooked it, you committed the product and the hours. From then on it's on a clock. Used in service, it's a sale. Left over and turned at the end of the week, it's a loss you already paid for. That's why over-prepping isn't "being ready" — it's spending money on stock you're hoping to sell. The tighter your prep is to what you'll actually use, the less of your money is sitting in a tray, betting on a busy service. Mise isn't readiness. It's inventory with a deadline.

## Küme 10 — Roster / personel

### 29. Overtime is a food-cost-level leak nobody tracks
Kitchens will argue over a few cents on a portion and never look at the overtime quietly stacking up every week. One person staying back an hour, a shift that always runs long, a section over-staffed on a quiet day — none of it feels like much in the moment. But labour is usually neck and neck with food as your biggest cost, and unlike food, the leak doesn't show up on a plate. It shows up on a wage bill you look at once a month, after it's already spent. The same attention you put on food cost — the portion, the yield, the waste — is worth putting on hours. An over-portioned plate and an over-staffed shift are the same mistake: paying for more than the work needed. One's just easier to see.

### 30. The shift you over-staff by one, every week
There's usually one shift a week with a person too many. Not a disaster — just one more than the work needed. It happens because the roster gets copied from last week, and last week's from the one before, and nobody re-checks it against what actually walks through the door on a Tuesday. One extra person, one shift, every week, is a salary's worth of hours over a year — spent on covers that were never going to need them. Rostering to the busy nights is easy; everyone feels those. The money is in the quiet shifts, where it's tempting to keep the team whole and easier not to think about it. Match the people to the covers, not to the habit. The over-staffed shift is the over-portioned plate of your wage bill.
