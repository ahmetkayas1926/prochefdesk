The Off-Site Catering Equipment Checklist

The job is won or lost at the pack-out, in the car park behind your kitchen, an hour before you leave. The food gets all the glory. But a missing extension lead or an empty gas bottle will sink an event faster than a mediocre canapé ever could.

By Ahmet Kaya 9 min July 2026

Cook in your own kitchen and everything you need is within arm's reach. You never think about the peeler, the second probe, the roll of blue tape — they're just there. Off-site catering strips that safety net away. You arrive at a marquee in a field, or a dry hire venue with a locked cupboard and one working socket, and the only kit you have is the kit you loaded. Forget something and there is no walk to the store cupboard. There is a forty-minute round trip, or there is doing without.

A proper catering equipment checklist is not a nice-to-have. It is the single document that separates caterers who look calm on-site from caterers who spend the first hour panicking. This is how I build mine, category by category, and how it scales with the cover count.

Why the pack-out is where events break

I have never lost an event because the food was wrong. I have come close to losing one because I packed the induction hobs but not the pans that fit them. I have watched a colleague plate ninety covers with two serving spoons because the third box never made it onto the van. The failure mode in catering is almost never culinary skill. It is logistics.

The reason is simple. The food is the thing you rehearsed, tasted, costed and stressed about for weeks. The equipment is the boring stuff you assume will sort itself out. It won't. Boxes get borrowed for the last job and not returned. Gas runs low without anyone noticing. A trolley wheel seizes. The only defence is a written list you run every single time, treated with the same seriousness as the menu.

The rule I live by: if it isn't on the list, it doesn't get loaded, and if it's on the list it gets physically ticked as it goes on the van — not "I'm pretty sure I packed it". Memory is where events go to die. A ticked box is a fact.

The pack-out list, by category

Break the list into functional stages, not into a giant undifferentiated pile. You pack in stages, you unload in stages, and you want to be able to look at a category and know at a glance that it is complete. Six categories cover almost everything: transport, holding, prep, service, safety, and the forgotten-items list that has its own section because it earns one.

1. Transport — getting it there intact

Everything else depends on this. The best kit in the world is useless in a heap on the floor of the van. Transport gear is what moves your load safely and lets one or two people shift it without a hernia.

  • Stackable crates with lids — colour-code or label them by station. A cook's crate, a service crate, a bar crate. Reusable crates beat cardboard every time; cardboard collapses when it's damp.
  • Flat-bed trolley and a sack truck — the sack truck for stacked crates and cambros, the flat-bed for the heavy cold boxes. A venue with a long corridor or a first-floor function room will punish you for skipping these.
  • Ratchet straps and bungees — to stop the load shifting on a roundabout. A cambro of hot braise tipping in the back is a category of bad day all its own.
  • Insulated cold boxes / cool boxes with ice packs — the cold chain is a legal and safety obligation, not a suggestion. Cold food must stay cold in transit.
  • Furniture blankets or old duvets — for wedging glassware and crockery so it doesn't chip on the drive.

The cold chain deserves a flag here because it is the one transport failure that becomes a food safety failure. The US FDA Food Code — the model most retail and food-service temperature rules descend from — puts cold holding at 5 °C (41 °F) or below and hot holding at 60 °C (135 °F) or above, and the transit window between your kitchen and the venue counts. Pack enough ice packs to hold temperature for the whole journey plus a two-hour buffer, and carry a probe to prove it.

2. Holding — keeping food at temperature on-site

Once you're there, the food sits in limbo between arrival and service. This is where most temperature failures happen and where a lot of caterers under-invest. Holding gear buys you a window.

  • Hot boxes / cabinets — electric where there's power, or well-insulated passive boxes for shorter windows. Know your window: a passive hot box holds safe temperature for maybe two to three hours, not all afternoon.
  • Cambros (insulated food carriers) — the workhorse. Hot on the way, cold on the way if you clean and swap. Bring more than you think; they double as transport and holding.
  • Chafing dishes with water pans and lids — one per hot dish on a buffet, plus a spare. The lid matters as much as the base for holding temperature.
  • Bain-marie or induction hob + gastronorm pans — for à la minute finishing and holding sauces.
  • Probe thermometer (listed again under safety, and no, that's not a mistake — you want one at the holding station and one at the pass).

3. Prep — the on-site kitchen you build from nothing

Assume the venue kitchen has nothing usable. Assume it, even when the booking sheet says "fully equipped" — because "fully equipped" means someone else's idea of equipped, and it will be missing exactly the thing you need. Pack a self-sufficient prep kit.

  • Colour-coded chopping boards — the full set. Cross-contamination control travels with you.
  • Your own knife roll — never rely on venue knives. Chef's knife, paring, serrated, a boning knife if the menu needs it, plus a steel.
  • Digital scales — for portioning to the recipe, not to the eye. Off-site is exactly where portion discipline slips and food cost quietly balloons.
  • Mixing bowls, jugs, ladles, tongs, spatulas, whisk — the small tools you never notice until they're absent.
  • Cutting-edge basics: tin opener, peeler, box grater, kitchen scissors, sieve. The cheap tools that stop a whole dish.
  • Cling film, foil, greaseproof, deli containers — for mise, for covering, for leftovers.
  • Trays, gastronorm pans and a couple of sheet pans — your work surface when the venue gives you one folding table.

4. Service — how it reaches the guest

The kitchen can be flawless and the guest still gets a cold plate handed over on a wobbly table. Service gear is the front-of-house half of the pack-out, and it's the half chefs habitually under-pack because it isn't "cooking".

  • Chafing fuel (gel or wick), and twice as much as the maths says — a tin of gel burns for around two hours. A five-hour buffet across four chafers needs a dozen tins, and you always run one longer than planned. Cheap insurance; run out and the food goes cold in front of guests.
  • Serving spoons, tongs, ladles, cake slices, serving forks — count one dedicated utensil per dish plus spares. This is the single most-forgotten service item in catering.
  • Crockery, cutlery, glassware — to the cover count plus a breakage margin (5–10%).
  • Platters, bowls, tiered stands, display boards — how the food actually looks on the table.
  • Table linen, skirting, napkins — if you're dressing the buffet as well as filling it.
  • Waiter's friend, service cloths, drinks trays — the small front-of-house kit.

5. Safety — non-negotiable, every event

This category never scales down. A ten-cover private dinner needs the same safety kit as a three-hundred-cover wedding, because the risks are the same and the law doesn't care about your guest count.

  • Probe thermometer plus a spare — and probe wipes. You are recording temperatures on-site, both for holding and for any cook-to-order.
  • First aid kit — burns dressing, blue plasters (detectable), the lot. Kitchens injure people; a field kitchen more so.
  • Disposable gloves, aprons, blue roll — in quantity.
  • Hand sanitiser and a hand-wash setup — venues often have no accessible sink near your prep area. A jerry can, a bowl, soap and paper towel is a legal hand-wash station.
  • Fire blanket and a small extinguisher — if you're running live gas.
  • Allergen matrix and labels — the paperwork that keeps someone safe and keeps you out of court.
  • Cleaning kit — sanitiser spray, cloths, bin bags, so you leave the venue as you found it.

6. The forgotten items — the box that saves the day

This is the list that separates the pro from the enthusiast. Everyone remembers knives and chafers. Almost nobody remembers the boring infrastructure until it's missing. Keep a permanent, pre-packed "essentials" crate that never gets unpacked between jobs — it just gets topped up.

  • Extension leads and a 4-way block or two — venues have one socket in the wrong place. This is the single most-forgotten item in off-site catering, and the most crippling.
  • Spare gas bottle (and the right regulator) — never leave with one bottle. Bottles run out mid-service and never at a convenient moment.
  • Bin bags — heavy duty, and more than you think — you generate a shocking amount of waste and the venue bins are locked or full.
  • Blue tape / gaffer tape / masking tape — labelling, securing leads across a walkway, holding a wobbly table leg. Tape fixes half the problems on-site.
  • Marker pens and labels — for allergen labels, day-dots, "this one's the vegan tray".
  • Matches / lighter / long lighter — for the chafing fuel you remembered but can't light.
  • Torch or head-torch — outdoor events in the dark, packing down after sunset.
  • Cable ties, string, a multi-tool, spare fuses, a roll of cling.
  • Water — drinking and cleaning — do not assume the venue has a working tap where you need it.
  • A printed run sheet and this checklist itself — on paper, because your phone will die.
The £30 crate that has saved more events than any single piece of kit: extension leads, a spare gas bottle, a roll of gaffer, bin bags, matches, a torch and a marker pen. Pack it once, never unpack it, top it up after every job. It weighs almost nothing and it has bailed me out more times than I can count.

Scaling the gear to the cover count

The safety kit is fixed. Everything else scales, and the mistake is to scale it linearly in your head and get it wrong under pressure. Some things scale with covers, some with dishes, some with service duration. Work out which axis each item lives on and the maths stops being guesswork.

Chafing fuel tins = chafers on the buffet × ⌈service hours ÷ 2⌉ + 2 spare

That formula alone stops the most common on-site failure. Four chafers over a five-hour buffet needs 4 × 3 + 2 = 14 tins, not the "half a box should do it" that leaves food cold at hour four.

The pack-out as a repeatable document

The list only works if it's the same list, every time, refined after every job. A one-off checklist scribbled the night before is barely better than memory. What you want is a living master list you clone per event, tick as you load, and — this is the part most people skip — debrief against afterwards.

After every event, spend five minutes on two questions: what did we bring that we never touched, and what did we have to improvise because it wasn't there? The first list gets you a lighter, cheaper van. The second gets added to the master immediately, while the pain is fresh. Do this for a season and your checklist becomes genuinely bulletproof — every gap you ever hit is already closed.

Keep the master with the menu, not in your head and not on a laminated card that lives in one person's bag. When the event details change — the count jumps, a dish gets swapped, the venue turns out to have no power — the equipment implications ripple out, and a list tied to the booking updates with them.

The point

Guests taste the food and remember the food. But the food only reaches them if the boring infrastructure holds — the leads, the gas, the tape, the ticked box. Off-site catering is a logistics discipline wearing a chef's jacket, and the checklist is the discipline made visible.

Write the list. Run it every time. Tick it physically. Debrief against it. Do that, and you'll be the caterer standing calm in the car park while everyone else is halfway back to the kitchen for the thing they forgot.

"You don't rise to the occasion at an off-site event. You fall to the level of your pack-out."

Nobody ever complimented a caterer on their extension leads. But the ones who bring them are the ones who get booked again.
