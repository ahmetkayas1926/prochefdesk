HACCP Temperature Log: What to Record and How Often

Ahmet Kaya · June 2026 · 8 min read

A fridge temperature log is the simplest record in the kitchen and the one most often failed at audit — not because the fridges are warm, but because the log has gaps, no corrective actions, or no signature. An auditor is not really checking your fridges. They are checking whether you can prove, on paper, that you have been checking them. Here is exactly what a temperature log has to capture, how often, and the one column most logs leave blank.

What the log is actually proving

HACCP is built on monitoring critical control points and keeping the records that show you did. Cold storage is a control point: fridges have to hold cold enough that bacteria do not multiply, freezers cold enough to stay frozen. The log is the evidence. When an auditor asks for your temperature records, they are testing one thing — can you produce a complete, signed, retrievable history for any date they pick? If you can, the conversation is short. If there are holes, it is not.

The seven columns a temperature log needs

A defensible daily log captures seven things. Anything less and the record cannot stand on its own.

ColumnWhy it has to be there
UnitWhich appliance — "Fridge 1", "Walk-in", "Freezer" — each logged separately
DateThe day, so the record is retrievable by date
Time / checkWhen it was read — typically a morning and an evening check
TemperatureThe actual reading, a real number, not a tick
In range?Pass or fail against that unit's safe band
Corrective actionWhat you did if it failed — the column that decides everything
InitialsWho took the reading — accountability

The safe bands the readings are checked against follow your jurisdiction's food code — a chilled unit at or below roughly 4–5 °C, a freezer at or below −18 °C are common targets. The exact numbers vary by region; the standards published in codes like the FDA Food Code are the reference your local rules build on. The log's job is to hold every reading against those bands.

How often to check

The widely-used standard is twice a day per cold unit — once near open, once near close. The morning check confirms the unit held overnight; the evening check confirms it held through service. Once a day misses a half-day of risk; twice a day brackets the working period at both ends. Some operations log more (high-risk units, delivery days), never fewer.

Two checks a day is the practical floor. Morning and evening bracket the whole working period. A single daily reading leaves a twelve-hour window unaccounted for — and a gap is exactly what an auditor looks for.

The corrective-action column is the one that matters

Here is the single most-cited gap on paper temperature logs: a reading was out of range, it was written down — and the "what we did about it" line was left blank. An out-of-range reading with a documented corrective action is a system working: you caught it, you acted, you recorded it. An out-of-range reading with nothing beside it is a hazard you noticed and ignored. The second is far worse than the temperature itself.

A corrective action does not have to be elaborate. "Door found ajar, closed, re-checked 30 min — 4°C" is a complete record. "Moved stock to backup unit, called engineer" is a complete record. The point is that the failure has a documented response attached to the same timestamp.

An out-of-range reading is not a fail. A blank corrective-action box is. Auditors expect occasional excursions — fridges have bad days. What they need to see is that you caught each one and acted. The failure is not the warm fridge; it is the silence next to it.

Why gaps are worse than failures

Most chefs worry about a high reading. Auditors worry about a missing one. A month with a couple of documented, corrected excursions reads as a control system that works. A month with five blank days reads as a control system that is not being run — and the auditor cannot tell whether those five days were fine or disastrous, which is the whole problem. Completeness beats perfection. A complete log with honest, corrected failures is stronger evidence than a suspiciously perfect log with holes in it.

Paper, spreadsheet, or app

A printed grid on a clipboard works — many excellent kitchens run on exactly that, and a blank monthly template you print and pin up is a perfectly good start. Its weaknesses are the human ones: a clipboard that goes missing takes the month with it, handwriting that an auditor cannot read is a reading that does not count, and nobody can tell at a glance whether a day was skipped until the gap is already permanent. A spreadsheet fixes legibility but still relies on someone remembering. The value of a digital log is not the recording — it is that it can flag the gap and the failure while you can still fix them, and produce the signed monthly record on demand.

What to do this week

  1. Make sure every cold unit is logged separately — one fridge that holds high should not hide behind another that runs cold.
  2. Move to twice a day if you are checking once.
  3. Never leave the corrective-action box blank on a failed reading. One honest line is the whole point.
  4. Check last month for gaps. A missing day you find yourself is fixable context; a missing day the auditor finds is a finding.

The point

A temperature log is not about the fridges. It is about proof. Seven columns, twice a day, every unit, with the corrective-action box filled in whenever a reading fails — and no gaps. Get those right and the audit conversation about cold storage is over before it starts. Leave holes in it and the warmest thing in the kitchen is the auditor's opinion of your records.
