How Much Food Per Person for Catering

Ahmet Kaya · June 2026 · 8 min read

"How much food per person?" is the question that decides whether you run short at a wedding or throw away a third of what you cooked. Both are expensive — one in reputation, one in cash. The honest answer is that there is no single number, but there are reliable starting points, and three variables that move them in predictable directions. Get the starting point and the three variables right and you will plan quantities you can trust.

Start with the three variables, not the number

Before any portion chart is useful, three things decide how much a guest actually eats:

  • Length of the event. A two-hour reception and a five-hour wedding are different appetites. People graze for as long as food is in front of them. Quantity scales with hours, not just heads.
  • Time of day. A lunch crowd eats less than a dinner crowd. A mid-afternoon event that straddles no mealtime eats least of all — people came for the occasion, not the meal.
  • Plated or self-serve. A plated meal is portion-controlled: you decide the gram weight. A buffet is appetite-controlled: guests serve themselves, and they take more than you would plate. Self-serve always needs more food per head than plated for the same satisfaction.

Every number below is a plated, dinner-time starting point. Push it up for buffets, long events, and evening service; ease it down for short, daytime, plated occasions.

Canapés and passed appetizers

Canapés are priced by the piece per guest per hour, and the count depends entirely on what follows.

ScenarioPieces per guest
Pre-dinner reception (meal to follow)3–5 pieces, first hour
Canapés as the meal (2–3 hr)10–14 pieces total
Each additional reception hour+2–3 pieces

The rule that matters: if canapés replace a meal, you are no longer counting snacks, you are building dinner out of small plates — cost and quantity both jump, and the variety has to carry the guest through the whole event.

Mains: the plated portion weights

For a plated main, portion by component. These are cooked, edible weights per guest — what lands on the plate, not what you purchased.

ComponentPer guest (plated, cooked)
Protein (meat / fish, boneless)140–180 g
Starch (potato, rice, pasta)110–150 g
Vegetables / salad80–120 g
Sauce30–60 ml
Buy more than the plate weight. These are cooked, edible grams. A protein that yields 70% after trimming and cooking means you purchase about 1.43× the plate weight — a 160 g portion starts as roughly 230 g raw. Plan quantities on the plate; plan purchasing on the yield. (More on this in ingredient yield percentage.)

Multiple proteins on a buffet

When guests can take both the chicken and the beef, they will. You do not plan each protein at a full portion — but nor do you simply split one portion across two. A practical rule: with two proteins offered, plan each at about 60–70% of a single-protein portion. Total protein per head ends up slightly above a single plated portion, because choice drives consumption up.

Sides, bread, and dessert

  • Buffet sides: 100–140 g per guest per side dish, dropping as the number of sides rises — more options, smaller average serving of each.
  • Bread: 1–2 pieces per guest for a plated meal; a basket per table.
  • Dessert: one plated portion per guest. For a dessert table or grazing finish, 2–3 small pieces per guest — people sample rather than commit.

Drinks

The standard planning assumption for a hosted bar: one drink per guest per hour, weighted to the first hour (people drink faster early). A four-hour evening event lands around 4–5 drinks per guest. Split it roughly to your crowd — a common starting split is 50% wine, 30% beer, 20% spirits/soft — then adjust for what you know about the event.

The buffer — and where it does not go

Plan a buffer, but plan it where running short hurts, not everywhere. A 5–10% buffer on the protein of a plated event is sensible — running out of the main is the unrecoverable failure. A blanket 20% buffer across every component is just waste with extra steps: you will bin most of it, and the cost lands on a job you have already priced.

Running short and over-catering are not equal mistakes. Running out of the main course at a wedding is a story the client tells for years. A little leftover salad is forgotten by Monday. Weight your buffer toward the dishes where a shortfall is visible and unrecoverable — the protein, the dessert — and keep it tight everywhere else.

Why portion control is also food safety

Over-catering does not only cost money — it creates a holding and leftover problem. Food cooked and not served sits in the danger zone while you decide what to do with it. Planning realistic quantities keeps less food in hot-holding for less time. For the temperatures that govern that window, public guidance like the U.S. government's food safety resources set the standards a catering operation has to hold to.

What to do this week

  1. Write down your own portions per guest for the dishes you cater most. You already know them in your hands; get them on paper so they scale reliably.
  2. Tag each as plated or self-serve — and bump the self-serve numbers.
  3. Buy on yield, plan on the plate. Keep the two numbers separate so purchasing is never light.
  4. Put the buffer on the protein, not on everything.

The point

There is no universal "food per person" number because a guest's appetite is set by the event, not by a chart. But the starting points are reliable, and the three variables — length, time of day, plated versus self-serve — move them in directions you can predict. Plan from those, weight your buffer toward the dishes where a shortfall is unrecoverable, and you will neither run short nor cook a third of the food into the bin.
