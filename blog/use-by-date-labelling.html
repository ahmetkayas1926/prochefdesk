Use-By vs Best-Before, and the Prep Labels That Pass Audit

Use-by and best-before are not two ways of saying the same thing. One is a safety line drawn in law; the other is a quality opinion. Confuse them on your prep labels and the inspector notices before you've finished shaking hands.

By Ahmet Kaya 8 min July 2026

Walk into any kitchen mid-audit and watch where the inspector's hand goes first. Not the paperwork folder. Not the fridge thermometer. The hand goes to a container of decanted cream, or a tub of cooked rice, or a vac-pack of confit, and it turns the thing around looking for a date. What's written there — and whether it says the right word — tells them more about how you run the place than anything you could say out loud.

So let's get the difference between use-by vs best-before straight, because most chefs use the terms loosely and then label prep loosely, and that's exactly the habit that turns a routine visit into a written non-conformance.

The legal difference, in one sentence each

Use-by is about safety. It goes on food that spoils in a way that can make someone ill — raw meat, dairy, cooked rice, prepared salads, anything that supports the growth of pathogens or the toxins they leave behind. After the use-by date the food may be dangerous even if it looks and smells fine. In the UK it is a legal offence to sell or use food after its use-by date. That is not a guideline; it is the law.

Best-before is about quality. It goes on shelf-stable and low-risk food — dried pasta, tinned tomatoes, flour, oil, most frozen goods. After best-before the food is still safe; it just may have lost texture, colour or flavour. Stale, not dangerous. The UK Food Standards Agency puts the split plainly: you can eat food up to the use-by date but not after, whereas best-before is about how good the food will be, not whether it's safe. Their guidance on best-before and use-by dates is the reference I point new commis at, and it settles most kitchen arguments in thirty seconds.

The US framing is looser but points the same way. The FDA and USDA don't legally mandate most consumer date labels (infant formula is the exception), and phrases like "best if used by", "sell by" and "use by" are largely about peak quality rather than a hard safety cutoff. That's fine for a sealed retail product on a shelf. It is not fine as your operating logic in a commercial kitchen, because the moment you open, cook or decant that product, the manufacturer's date stops applying and the clock you own starts running.

The trap: the manufacturer's date is for the food as sold, sealed. A tub of crème fraîche best-before three weeks out means nothing once you've cracked the lid and spooned half of it into a piping bag. From that moment it's your date, your responsibility, and your label. The printed date on the pot is now irrelevant to your safe shelf-life.

The made-on plus day-dot system

Here's the system that survives audits, and it's boring on purpose. Every item that is opened, decanted, cooked, thawed or prepped in-house gets a label the moment it changes state. The label carries two things that matter and one that helps:

  • What it is — "Cooked chicken", "Opened double cream", "Diced onion". Named, not guessed.
  • Made-on / opened-on date — the day it entered your control. Some chefs add the time for high-risk items, and for cooked-and-cooled food you should.
  • Use-by date — the last safe day, calculated from the made-on date plus the shelf-life you've assigned that item.

The day-dot is the physical form of this. A coloured, dissolvable sticker — different colour per day of the week in a lot of operations, or simply a printed date label — slapped on at the point of prep. The colour lets a section chef read the fridge at a glance without picking up every tub. The written date is what makes it defensible.

Use-by date = Made-on / opened-on date + Assigned shelf-life

Notice what this does: it converts a vague best-before on the original packaging into a hard, in-house use-by that you can prove you calculated and applied consistently. That consistency is the thing auditors actually score. A kitchen where every day-dot follows the same rule looks controlled. A kitchen where cooked rice from Monday and cooked rice from Thursday carry different-looking labels — or none — looks like a coin toss.

Shelf-life once opened: the numbers that matter

The single most useful piece of paper in a walk-in is a shelf-life chart pinned to the door, because it removes the argument. People stop guessing and start reading. Assign a shelf-life to each class of prepped food and hold everyone to it. These are common commercial defaults — check them against your own local code and your own HACCP plan, because jurisdictions vary and your risk assessment overrides any generic table:

Two things chefs get wrong here. First, the prep day counts as day one — "two days" doesn't mean two full days plus the day you made it. Cook the chicken Monday, it's gone by end of Wednesday, not Thursday. Second, the shelf-life you assign in-house can only ever be shorter than the original best-before, never longer. Opening a pot of yoghurt with a three-week best-before doesn't buy you three weeks; it buys you three days from the crack of the lid, and if the original date lands sooner, the original wins.

And the decision has to be conservative because you can't smell your way to safety on a use-by item. The sniff test is a best-before tool — fine for whether a loaf is stale. It is worthless against Listeria in cooked, chilled food, which grows slowly at fridge temperature and gives you no warning odour at all. That is the whole reason use-by exists as a separate legal category: some dangers are invisible.

Where date labelling and FIFO become the same job

FIFO — first in, first out — is the other half of this, and the two systems only work bolted together. A date label tells you when something must go. FIFO is the shelving discipline that makes sure the oldest safe stock is the one your hand reaches first, so nothing quietly ages out at the back of the shelf while fresh stock gets used off the front.

The mechanics are simple and physical:

  • New stock loads to the back; older, dated stock rotates to the front.
  • The day-dot faces out, so the earliest use-by is readable without unstacking.
  • Anything past its in-house use-by is pulled and logged as waste, not shuffled back "to check later".

When this runs properly, the date label stops being a compliance chore and becomes an inventory signal. The tub creeping toward its use-by is the tub you build tomorrow's special around. Labelling discipline and waste control turn out to be the same habit viewed from two angles — one protects the guest, the other protects the margin.

The label on the tub is only half the record — the other half is proving the cook and cool that preceded it. The HACCP logs hold your cook & cool temperatures and receiving checks, so when an inspector traces a cooked-and-cooled item back from its day-dot, the date on the tub and the temperature log line up on the same timeline instead of living in two different books.

What the auditor actually checks

Having sat on both sides of these visits, the pattern is consistent. The inspector isn't trying to trip you up on trivia. They're testing whether your labelling reflects a real, repeatable system or a tidy-up done that morning. In rough order, they look for:

  1. Is high-risk prep dated at all? An undated tub of cooked chicken is an automatic finding. No date means no control, and no argument.
  2. Does the label use the right word for the risk? A use-by item carrying a casual "best before" label reads as a kitchen that doesn't understand its own hazards.
  3. Is anything past its own use-by still in the fridge? One expired tub behind the fresh stock undoes a spotless folder of paperwork. This is where broken FIFO gets exposed.
  4. Do the dates tell a consistent story? If the day-dot says cooked Tuesday but the cook-cool log has no Tuesday entry, that mismatch is worse than either gap alone — it looks like the label was invented.
  5. Is the shelf-life logic written down? A chart on the walk-in door showing your assigned shelf-lives turns "the chef decides" into "the system decides". Auditors love a documented rule far more than a confident chef.
The finding that costs you: it's rarely the missing label alone. It's the contradiction — a use-by date that doesn't match the temperature record, a best-before word on a safety item, a Monday day-dot on food you clearly made today. Contradictions signal a system that isn't real, and that's what escalates a note into an enforcement action.

Building the habit so it holds under pressure

None of this survives a Friday-night rush on willpower. It survives because the tools are at the station and the rule is one everybody knows without thinking. Practically:

  • Labels and a marker live at the prep bench, not in an office drawer. Friction is the enemy of compliance. If dating a tub means walking to find a pen, it won't happen at 18:45.
  • One shelf-life rule per food class, printed and pinned. Not per chef, not per mood.
  • Date at the moment of change of state — the instant you open, cook, cool or decant — never "later when it's quiet", because quiet never comes and later you'll guess.
  • The day-dot faces out. A label you have to unstack to read is a label nobody reads.
  • Pull-and-log, don't shuffle. Past its use-by, it leaves the fridge and enters the waste record. That record is also how you find out which prep you're consistently over-producing.

Get those five in place and the audit becomes the easy part, because you're not performing compliance for a visitor — you're just showing them the way you already work. The inspector turns the tub around, reads a clear name and a sensible use-by, cross-checks it against a temperature log that agrees, and moves on.

The point

Use-by is a safety cut-off written in law. Best-before is a quality opinion. The manufacturer owns the date on the sealed pack; the moment you open or cook it, you own the date, and the only honest way to own it is a made-on-plus-shelf-life label applied the instant the food changes state.

Do that consistently, bolt it to FIFO so the oldest safe stock moves first, and keep the shelf-life logic on the wall where anyone can read it. The day-dot on your decanted prep stops being the thing you dread the inspector finding and becomes the thing that proves, without you saying a word, that you know exactly what's in your fridge and exactly how long it has left.

"The date on the pot is the manufacturer's promise. The day-dot is yours — and yours is the one that gets checked."
